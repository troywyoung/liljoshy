# Project Name

Name the project to replace "liljoshy" placeholder with a production-ready name. Must appeal to developers, fit the agentic movement, and be interesting and disruptive.

## Context

This project uses dust for planning and documentation in AI agent workflows. The brand voice is direct, technical, practical, and confident — like a Unix utility.

Key constraints:
- Must appeal to developers
- Must fit with the agentic movement
- Cannot be "dust" (already taken)
- Needs to be interesting and a little disruptive
- Must align with brand voice: direct, technical, practical, confident

Brand voice guidelines prohibit words like: revolutionize, unlock, empower, cutting-edge, seamless, robust, powerful.

## Research Context

Current agentic AI coding tool landscape in 2026:
- Naming trends favor autonomous, workflow-oriented language
- Top tools: Claude Code, Cursor, Codex, Windsurf, Cline, Replit
- "Agentic coding" = AI-as-worker paradigm (not AI-as-assistant)
- "Vibe coding" = natural language descriptions over traditional coding
- Movement from conversational AI to autonomous agents handling entire workflows

Patterns observed:
- Single-word names common (Cursor, Codex, Cline, Windsurf)
- IDE/editor references (Windsurf = "agentic IDE")
- Action/motion words (Replit's "Idea to app, fast")
- Technical but approachable

## Naming Categories to Consider

### 1. Tool/Craft Names
Unix-style tool names that suggest precision and craftsmanship:
- forge, foundry, anvil, lathe, chisel
- mill, kiln, loom, press
- bench, jig, vice, clamp

### 2. Process/Flow Names
Names suggesting workflow, execution, or systematic work:
- flow, flux, stream, current
- cycle, loop, orbit, pivot
- step, stride, pace, cadence

### 3. Structure/Architecture Names
Names emphasizing organization and structure:
- scaffold, frame, lattice, grid
- spine, core, kernel, nexus
- stack, queue, heap, tree

### 4. Agent/Actor Names
Names suggesting autonomous action:
- agent, actor, worker, runner
- envoy, proxy, delegate, deputy
- pilot, operator, handler, executor

### 5. Disruptive/Unconventional Names
Names that are interesting and slightly disruptive:
- glitch, spike, pulse, surge
- riff, jam, groove, beat
- spark, flare, flash, bolt

## Open Questions

### What naming category best fits the project positioning?

#### Option: Tool/Craft Names
- Pros: Unix heritage, developer-friendly, implies precision and reliability
- Cons: Risk feeling traditional or generic, many good ones already taken
- Examples: forge, anvil, chisel, bench

#### Option: Process/Flow Names
- Pros: Aligns with "Flow state for AI coding agents" tagline, suggests continuous work
- Cons: May feel abstract, "flow" itself might be too on-the-nose
- Examples: flux, stride, cadence, orbit

#### Option: Structure/Architecture Names
- Pros: Emphasizes the core value prop (structure for agents), technical feel
- Cons: Could feel static rather than active, may not convey autonomous agent aspect
- Examples: scaffold, lattice, spine, frame

#### Option: Agent/Actor Names
- Pros: Directly communicates the agentic nature, clear positioning
- Cons: Crowded category, risk being too literal, less distinctive
- Examples: envoy, proxy, operator, pilot

#### Option: Disruptive/Unconventional Names
- Pros: Memorable, differentiating, fits "interesting and disruptive" requirement
- Cons: May sacrifice clarity, could feel gimmicky if not authentic
- Examples: glitch, riff, pulse, spark

### Should the name be a real word or invented?

#### Option: Real English word
- Pros: Easy to spell, pronounce, remember; instant meaning/associations
- Cons: Harder to find available domains, trademark issues, less unique
- Examples: Most Unix tools (grep, sed, awk), modern tools (Cursor, Windsurf)

#### Option: Invented/portmanteau word
- Pros: Unique, brandable, domains likely available
- Cons: Requires explanation, risk feeling artificial, harder to remember initially
- Examples: GitHub, GitLab, Replit

#### Option: Modified real word
- Pros: Balance of familiarity and uniqueness, hints at meaning
- Cons: Can feel trendy (dropped vowels, tech spelling), may age poorly
- Examples: Tumblr, Flickr (though this style feels dated in 2026)

### How literal vs. metaphorical should the name be?

#### Option: Literal/descriptive
- Pros: Immediately clear what it does, aids discovery, professional
- Cons: Less memorable, harder to differentiate, limits future pivots
- Examples: Claude Code, Copilot (describe their function)

#### Option: Metaphorical
- Pros: More memorable, room for storytelling, can grow with product
- Cons: Requires marketing to explain, may confuse initially
- Examples: Windsurf (suggests navigating/riding currents), Cursor (suggests precise control)

#### Option: Abstract/evocative
- Pros: Highly distinctive, emotional connection, brand flexibility
- Cons: Meaning is learned not inherent, requires strong execution
- Examples: Apple, Spotify (arbitrary but strong through usage)

### Should the name reference the Unix/CLI heritage?

#### Option: Embrace Unix heritage
- Pros: Aligns with brand voice ("like a Unix utility"), appeals to technical audience
- Cons: May limit perception to CLI tool, risks feeling dated
- Characteristics: Short, lowercase, cryptic-but-memorable (grep, awk, sed, vim)

#### Option: Modern developer tool vibe
- Pros: Broader appeal, contemporary feel, more approachable
- Cons: Less distinctive in crowded market, may lose technical credibility
- Characteristics: Single word, clean, professional (Cursor, Linear, Notion)

#### Option: Hybrid approach
- Pros: Technical roots with modern polish, appeals to both audiences
- Cons: Risk not fully committing to either positioning
- Characteristics: Real word with CLI sensibility (git, npm, yarn)

### What length works best?

#### Option: Ultra-short (2-4 letters)
- Pros: Fast to type, memorable, Unix-like, great for CLI
- Cons: Nearly impossible to find available domains, very limited options
- Examples: git, npm, vim, sed, awk

#### Option: Short (5-7 letters)
- Pros: Balance of brevity and availability, still CLI-friendly
- Cons: Competitive space for good names
- Examples: dust, forge, cursor, claude

#### Option: Medium (8-10 letters)
- Pros: More options available, room for meaning
- Cons: Longer to type, may feel less "utility-like"
- Examples: windsurf, scaffold, operator

### Should the name work as a verb?

#### Option: Noun that verbs well
- Pros: Natural CLI usage ("git commit", "dust check"), conversational
- Cons: Limits naming options significantly
- Examples: "Let's forge this feature", "Stream the task", "Frame the requirements"

#### Option: Noun-only
- Pros: Wider naming options, can still use with commands
- Cons: May feel less action-oriented
- Examples: "Run cursor on this", "Use claude for this"
